Definition 1
A Markov chain is an integer-time process, {Xn, n ⩾ 0}, for which the
sample values for each rv. Xn, n ⩾ 1, lie in a countable set S and depend
on the past only through the most recent rv. Xn−1. More specifically , for
all positive integers n, and for all choices of i, j, . . . , ℓ in S,
P{Xn = j|Xn−1 = i, Xn−2 = k, . . . , X0 = ℓ} = P{Xn = j|Xn−1 = i}
for all conditioning events Xn−1 = i, Xn−2 = k, . . . , X0 = ℓ of positive
probability .

§ Introduction
§ Introduction
Often we write the condition as follow
P{Xn|Xn−1, . . . , X0} = P{Xn|Xn−1}.
The possible values of Xn are called states. The state at a given time
summarizes everything about the past that is relevant to the future .
Furthermore, P{Xn = j|Xn−1 = i} depends only on i and j (not n) and is
denoted by
P{Xn = j|Xn−1 = i} = Pij.
Some people call this a homogeneous Markov chain.
For a non-homogeneous Markov chain, it satisfies
P{Xn = j|Xn−1 = i} = Pij(n).

§ Classification of States
§ Classification of States
Definition 6
For finite-state Markov chains, a recurrent state is a state i that is accessible from
all states that are accessible from i (i is recurrent if i → j implies that j → i). A
transient state is a state that is not recurrent.
Remark: If a Markov chain ever enters a recurrent state, it returns to that state
eventually with probability 1, and thus keeps returning infinitely often. In fact,
this essentially serves as the definition of recurrence for Markov chains with
countably-infinite state spaces.

§ Classification of States
§ Classification of States
Theorem 1
For finite-state Markov chains, either all states in a class are transient or
all are recurrent.
Proof:
Assume that i is transient (i.e., for some j, i → j but j ↛ i) and
suppose that i and m are in the same class (i.e., i ↔ m).
Then m → i and i → j, so m → j.
If j → m, then the walk from j to m could be extended to i; this is a
contradiction, and therefore there is no walk from j to m, and m is
transient.

Theorem 2
For any Markov chain (with either a finite or countably-infinite number of states),
all states in the same class have the same period.
Proof: Let i and j be any distinct pair of states in a class C. Then i ↔ j and
there is some n such that Pn
ij > 0 and some m such that Pm
ji > 0.
Since there is a walk of length n + m going from i to j and back to i, n + m
must be divisible by d(i).
Let t be any integer such that Pt
jj > 0. Since there is a walk of length
n + t + m from i to j, then back to j, and then to i, n + t + m is divisible by
d(i), and thus t is divisible by d(i).
Since this is true for every t such that Pt
jj > 0, d(j) is divisible by d(i).
Reversing the roles of i and j, d(i) is divisible by d(j), so d(i) =d(j).
