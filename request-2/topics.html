    Request 2: Lecture Alignment¶

    SYSU Stochastic Processes ▶ CMU 15-857 Analytical Performance Modeling and Design of Computer Systems

    • CMU — 15-857 Analytical Performance Modeling and Design of Computer Systems

      Focus. Introduces analytic stochastic modeling for computer-system performance.

      Topics: (1) Operational Laws (Little's Law, response-time law, asymptotic bounds, performance metrics); (2) Markov-chain theory (DTMC, CTMC, renewal theory, time-reversibility); (3) Poisson process (memorylessness, Bernoulli splitting, uniformity, PASTA); (4) Queueing theory (M/M/1, M/M/k, M/M/k/k, M/G/1, M/G/k, G/G/1, transform analysis); (5) Networks of queues (Jackson, class-Jackson); (6) Simulations (time vs ensemble averages, RV generation, Inspection Paradox); (7) Heavy-tailed empirical workloads; (8) Server-farm management (provisioning, power management, routing); (9) Scheduling analysis (FCFS, priorities, PS, LCFS, FB, SJF, PSJF, SRPT, SOAP, Gittins / Whittle indices); (10) Datacenter applications (multiserver scheduling, c-µ rule, parallel jobs).

      Objectives. Equip students to derive and apply analytic models for the design, capacity-planning, and scheduling of modern computer and datacenter systems, including reading current performance-research literature.

    • SYSU — Stochastic Processes

      Focus. Provides a comprehensive introduction to probability models and stochastic processes for engineering applications. Topics include probability bounds and limits, Poisson and renewal processes, discrete- and continuous-time Markov chains, queueing theory, and Markov decision processes. Emphasizes mathematical rigor and engineering applications (communication systems, operations research, reinforcement learning).

      Objectives.

      1. Build a rigorous foundation of probability and random-process theory.
      2. Model random systems using Poisson and Markov processes.
      3. Analyze steady-state and transient behavior of stochastic systems.
      4. Apply Markov decision processes and dynamic programming for stochastic-control problems.
      5. Use simulation for queueing and MDP systems.

    Following Prof. Harchol-Balter's two CHOICE POINTs

    Her published organization page makes the graduate-class syllabus explicitly branched via two decision points:

    1. CHOICE POINT 1 (after Chpt 15) — cover networks of queues, or skip straight to Chpt 20. Chpts 16–18 are listed under "Week 9: This is an optional week"; Chpt 19 is explicitly marked "skip 19" on either branch.
    2. CHOICE POINT 2 (after Chpt 24) — cover transforms, or skip them. Mor writes verbatim: "I recommend that you skip transforms the first time that you're teaching" → Chpts 25–27 are out.

    Chpt 21 (matrix-analytic) is also marked "This is optional and can be skipped as well". The scored core above follows the "skip both" path Mor herself recommends as default — the canonical 22-lecture core that a transfer student is expected to have mastered. The off-path chapters (16–19, 21, 25–27) are listed below for completeness.

    Note on the Chpts 28–30 / 31–32 grouping

    Mor's organization page assigns Chpts 28–30 to Week 12 and Chpts 31–32 to Week 13 as two consecutive instructional units on "Scheduling in M/G/1". They are grouped here because the SYSU evidence is identical across each unit: none of the specific size-based policies (PS / SJF / SRPT in 28–30; PSJF / SOAP / age-based in 31–32) is derived at SYSU, which covers FCFS plus the MDP optimal-control framework that generates these policies as derived results. Splitting Chpts 28–30 and 31–32 into 5 individual rows would only add 3 redundant Conceptual entries with the same evidence — Mor herself treats each block as a single weekly teaching unit.

    Beyond the 15-857 syllabus — extra SYSU material¶

    A pure CMU-side mapping (above) understates the SYSU course. The following SYSU material is not part of 15-857 at all, and demonstrates that the SYSU course is a rigorous, broader, graduate-level treatment of stochastic processes:

    SYSU topic Slides Why it strengthens the equivalency case
    Four-mode convergence typology (a.s. / r-th mean / probability / distribution) SP03 p.10–13 Rigorous foundation that 15-857 only assumes (Chpt 3 is a recitation review)
    Markov / Chebyshev / Chernoff inequalities (with proofs) SP03 p.6–8 Tail-bound prerequisites for renewal-LLN proofs; not derived in 15-857
    Wald's equality (Theorem 2) + generalized Wald (Theorem 3) SP15 p.12–24 More general stopping-time machinery than 15-857's M/G/1 treatment
    Renewal-reward theorem (general statement, multiple worked examples) SP14 p.33–49 Underpins inspection paradox, age, residual life, busy periods
    Markov decision processes — finite + infinite horizon SP10 – SP12 (3 chapters, ~138 pages) Bellman equations, value iteration, policy iteration; appears in 15-857 only implicitly via Gittins/Whittle
    Approximate dynamic programming + reinforcement learning SP13 Not in 15-857 syllabus at all
